\section{Online Schema Alignment}\label{sec:method}

Schema alignment requires the definition of a set of rules.
Therefore, we chose to draw on the \gls{sssom} specification\footnote{\url{https://mapping-commons.github.io/sssom/}}~\cite{matentzoglu2022simple} to structure and formalize these rules.
We developed a simplified version\footnote{Vocabulary: \url{https://onlineschemaalignmentltqp.github.io/vocabulary/vocab.ttl} -- Example: \url{https://onlineschemaalignmentltqp.github.io/vocabulary/example.ttl}} of \gls{sssom} and use the concept of \emph{subweb}~\cite{traveling_map_ltqp} to specify the domain of applicability of the schema alignment rules.
In this way, the rules apply only within a designated set of IRIs.
We also note that as logical contradictions are not possible, we can leave that out of scope.

Our system operates under the open-world assumption, in which multiple new \glspl{kg} and schema alignment rules may be discovered during processing.
To ensure robustness, the system must prevent infinite recursive rule applications.
When such situations occur, it should ideally resolve them without aborting the entire computation.
Since rules are applied within specific domains the likelihood of infinite recursion becomes minimal.
In cases where infinite recursion is detected or overlap of subwebs, the simple resolution strategy of rejecting the newly generated schema alignment rule is applied.

Another important consideration is the potential misuse, overspecification, and underspecification of ontology terms~\cite{abedjan2012reconciling, lorey2011rdf}.
In our use case, the goal is to enable querying data across the web under the assumption that data providers best understand their own data models and the intended entailments.
Thus, we scope the rules to non-overlapping subwebs to prevent unintended entailments.
However, we also allow query issuers to define additional rules that may interact with those provided by data publishers.
This is justified by the fact that query issuers can more easily track and manage the consequences of their own entailment definitions, whereas it would be unrealistic to expect data providers to anticipate the broader implications of their rules beyond the scope of their own datasets or use cases.

To discover alignment rules, we modify the traversal policy to follow object terms of triples with predicate IRI \texttt{semmap:ruleSetLocation}, where the dereferenced term provides a rule set.
When the engine encounters a rule set at step $s_i$, it applies the rules to its internal \gls{kg} $G_{s_i}$ via forward chaining, producing an aligned graph $G^{\prime}_{s_i}$.
The triples of $G^{\prime}_{s_i}$, rather than $G_{s_i}$, are passed to the traversal policy and inserted into the internal knowledge base for join processing, as traversal policies are not designed to handle vocabulary mismatches.
