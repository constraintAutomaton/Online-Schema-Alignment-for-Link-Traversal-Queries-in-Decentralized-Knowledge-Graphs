\section{Introduction}

Integrating multiple \glspl{kg} presents scalability and heterogeneity challenges.
Scalability constraints due to data size, decentralization of data due to its management by different entities, or privacy often necessitate federated querying or link traversal-based querying~\cite{Hartig2012} rather than materialization.
Heterogeneity in knowledge representation and vocabulary poses another major challenge, as query issuers expect to formulate queries under a single, consistent representation.
Schema alignment aims to define rules that map knowledge expressed in one vocabulary to a semantically equivalent representation in another.
We propose that schema alignment can serve as a tool to simplify the formulation of such queries.
Schema alignment can be viewed as a subset of the problem of reasoning.
Prior work has investigated reasoning over SPARQL endpoints~\cite{terdjimi2016hylar}, RDF streams~\cite{bonte2025languages}, and schema alignment in SPARQL systems~\cite{cheng2023considering, Joshi2012, maarten2023pod}.
However, existing approaches assume alignment rules are known and scoped a priori.
Our work introduces \textit{online schema alignment}, where schema mappings are discovered, scoped, and applied dynamically during query execution of \gls{ltqp}~\cite{Hartig2012}.
\Gls{ltqp} is a query paradigm that exploits the fact that IRI in triples can be dereferenced to discover \glspl{kg} containing further information about a given term~\cite{linked_data}.
It is particularly suited to unindexed distributed networks, where different providers often express semantically equivalent properties using heterogeneous vocabularies.
Even trivial inconsistencies (e.g., HTTP vs. HTTPS schema.org vocabularies) require query rewriting, like inserting \texttt{UNION} clauses.
However, this approach has critical limitations,
as it assumes query issuers know all relevant alignment rules across large decentralized networks,
and that rules apply globally,
which is unrealistic given ontology design complexity and that mistakes can be made by data providers~\cite{abedjan2012reconciling, lorey2011rdf}.
Deeper semantic mismatches further complicate matters.
For example, providers may conceptualize responses as "replies" or "comments", or represent attributes like gender or city names as literals or IRI.
Rule chaining compounds the problem by requiring either materializing intermediate \glspl{kg} through multiple \texttt{CONSTRUCT} queries or backchaining to materialize a final query~\cite{maarten2023pod}.
Both methods are incompatible with \gls{ltqp}.
Executing multiple queries removes awareness of already-traversed documents, while backchaining into a single final query fails to discover intermediate resources needed for traversing the required search domain.
